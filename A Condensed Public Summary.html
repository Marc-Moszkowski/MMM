THE DEEPGULF AFFAIR — A CONDENSED PUBLIC SUMMARY

by Marc Moszkowski, Founder and Former CEO of DeepGulf, Inc.

Origins and Promise (2004–2008):

DeepGulf, Inc. was founded in 2004 to commercialize innovative patented technologies developed by Marc Moszkowski, who served as its CEO and sole engineer. For its first three years, Moszkowski funded operations entirely out of pocket, while the company’s Chairman, Rustin Howard, failed to raise promised capital.

In 2008, after Moszkowski independently secured multimillion-dollar contracts in East Timor, Howard belatedly raised $650,000—mostly from friends and relatives—in exchange for 10% of DeepGulf’s equity.

Exploitation and Financial Obfuscation (2008–2013)

Despite revenues generated overseas, DeepGulf’s internal finances became opaque. Moszkowski was repeatedly denied access to financial records and went unpaid for 7 of the company’s 12 active years—even as DeepGulf certified to U.S. immigration authorities that he would be paid a $132,000 salary, personally guaranteed by Howard using real estate assets.

To keep the company afloat, Moszkowski redirected $125,000 in personal consulting income to DeepGulf. In return, Howard unilaterally replaced Moszkowski’s unpaid salary with a fabricated 17% “loan,” recharacterizing his own money as debt—without board authorization or shareholder disclosure.

Betrayal and Retaliation (2014–2017)

In 2014, Howard attempted to fraudulently list himself as co-inventor on a patent solely authored by Moszkowski. In 2016, after years of dysfunction and silence, Moszkowski proposed that Howard step aside and let committed external sponsors assume control. Instead, Howard retaliated by cutting off Moszkowski’s U.S. visa support and falsely accusing him of embezzlement.

The Fabricated Lawsuit (2017–2018):

Forced into exile in 2017, Moszkowski was sued from afar in Florida state court for $1.3 million plus double damages. The suit was based on distortions, omissions, and fundamental contradictions:

1. The statute of limitations had long expired;

2. DeepGulf owed Moszkowski over $1 million in unpaid salary and expenses;

3. There was no theft—only documented support from Moszkowski to DeepGulf.

Judicial Manipulation and Forum Shopping (2018–2024)

After the case was dismissed in U.S. federal court in 2020, Howard used a defunct Timorese company—Toke Oil and Gas S.A.—as an artificial co-plaintiff. This move destroyed diversity jurisdiction and reversed the federal ruling.

In parallel, in France, one of Howard’s allies initiated a second suit and obtained a lien on Moszkowski’s only asset—his ancestral home—based on a fictitious, usurious “loan.” In truth, the funds had never been transferred by the claimant and represented Moszkowski’s own unpaid earnings.

Silence, Obstruction, and Proxy Abuse

Although Moszkowski informed more than 40 shareholders of the unfolding events, none responded constructively. Howard retained full control over company records, bank accounts, and shareholder communications. The combination of silence, withheld access, and potential proxy misuse raised red flags about internal misappropriation and fictitious investor representation.

The Current Situation (2025)

Now 71, Moszkowski lives in forced exile, destitute, without means of transportation, physically unfit due to two large untreated hernias, and in absolute solitude 5,000 miles from the court. His French bank accounts are frozen. His ancestral home is being seized. The Florida court denied him a remote trial appearance and entered a default judgment in his inevitable absence—without hearing, without evidence, and without legal justification—despite over 150 detailed filings backed by unrebutted evidence.

The Court awarded $1,035,000 in treble damages over an alleged $345,000 transfer—money that was not paid by DeepGulf, but rather voluntarily sent by East Timorese partners to help cover Moszkowski’s tax burden in France. The payment was disclosed in writing to Howard in 2010, 2011, 2012, and 2014. There was no evidence of harm, standing, or legal theory to support the claim—yet judgment was rendered.

Moszkowski’s home in France has already been ordered seized by a French court, acting on a default judgment obtained by a co-litigant of DeepGulf, based on the same disputed funds. The property is scheduled for auction. Even if the Florida judgment is overturned, the French process will not pause or reverse. Appeal will not stay execution. He will be thrown onto the pavement—stripped of shelter, income, and any remaining recourse. His dogs, the only companions who have stood by him, will be euthanized. The last vestige of a lifetime of effort—technical, intellectual, and moral—will be extinguished. He appeals not for pity, but for justice, in what may be the final moment before irrevocable loss.
