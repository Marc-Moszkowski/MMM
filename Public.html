THE DEEPGULF AFFAIR — A CONDENSED PUBLIC SUMMARY

by Marc Moszkowski, Founder and Former CEO of DeepGulf, Inc.

(Cliquer ici pour la version en français)

 

Origins and Promise (2004–2008):

In 2004, DeepGulf, Inc. was founded in Pensacola, Florida, to commercialize innovative patented technologies developed by Marc Moszkowski, who served as its CEO and sole engineer. For its first three years, Moszkowski funded operations entirely out of pocket, while the company’s Chairman, Rustin Howard, failed to raise promised capital.

In 2008, after Moszkowski independently secured multimillion-dollar contracts in East Timor, Howard belatedly raised $650,000—mostly from friends and relatives—in exchange for 10% of DeepGulf’s equity.

Exploitation and Financial Obfuscation (2008–2013)

Despite revenues generated overseas, DeepGulf’s internal finances became opaque. Moszkowski was denied access to financial records and went unpaid for 7 of the company’s 12 active years—even as Howard certified to U.S. immigration authorities that Moszkowski would be paid a $132,000 annual salary, personally guaranteed by Howard using his real estate assets. Moszkowski is a foreign citizen and was present in the United States on an H1-B visa.

To keep the company able to pay his salary, Moszkowski redirected $125,000 in personal consulting income to DeepGulf. In return, Howard unilaterally recharacterized a $50,000 salary obligation as a 17% third party “loan”, without board authorization or shareholder disclosure, and spent the $125,000 received from Moszkowski exclusively on his own expenses.

Betrayal and Retaliation (2014–2017)

In 2014, Howard attempted to fraudulently list himself as co-inventor on a patent solely authored by Moszkowski. In 2016, after years of dysfunction and silence, Moszkowski proposed that Howard step aside and let committed external sponsors assume control. Instead, Howard retaliated by cutting off Moszkowski’s U.S. visa support and falsely accusing him of embezzlement.

The Fabricated Lawsuit (2017–2018):

Forced into exile in 2017, Moszkowski was sued from afar in Florida state court for $1.3 million plus double damages. The suit was based on distortions, omissions, and fundamental contradictions:

1. There was no legal resolution by the Board of Directors to sue;

2. The statute of limitations had long expired;

3. All of the evidence was either blatantly misconstrued, or was poorly fabricated;

4. DeepGulf owed Moszkowski over $1 million plus interest in unpaid salary and expenses;

5. There was no theft—only documented support from Moszkowski to DeepGulf.

Judicial Manipulation and Forum Shopping (2018–2024)

After the case was dismissed in U.S. federal court in 2020, Howard used a defunct Timorese company—Toke Oil and Gas S.A.—as an artificial co-plaintiff. This move destroyed diversity jurisdiction and remanded the case to Florida State court.

In parallel, in France, one of Howard’s allies initiated a second lawsuit. Although initially dismissed, he filed again in a different venue and immediately obtained a lien on Moszkowski’s only asset—his ancestral home—based on the fictitious, usurious “loan.” In reality, the funds were never transferred by the claimant and instead represented Moszkowski’s own due salary, paid, like all salaries, by DeepGulf’s Chairman, Howard. The plaintiff failed to properly summon Moszkowski to the trial; the hearing date was deliberately withheld and only discovered two months after judgment. As a result, the ruling was entered by default, in absentia, without Moszkowski ever being given the opportunity to be heard.

Silence, Obstruction, and Proxy Abuse

Although Moszkowski informed more than 40 shareholders of the unfolding events in ten detailed and documented letters, none responded constructively. Howard retained full control over company records, bank accounts, and shareholder communications. The combination of silence, withheld access, and potential proxy misuse raised red flags about internal misappropriation and fictitious investor representation.

The Current Situation (2025)

Now 71, Moszkowski lives in forced exile, destitute, without means of transportation, physically unfit due to two large untreated hernias, and in absolute solitude 5,000 miles from the court. His French bank accounts are frozen by his opponents. His ancestral home is under seizure.

The Florida court denied Moszkowski’s request for remote trial appearance despite unrebutted medical evidence of physical incapacity and indigency. The court ordered him to appear in person—5,000 miles away in Pensacola. Upon his inevitable nonappearance, it entered default judgment in absentia—without hearing, without evidence, and without legal justification—ignoring over 150 detailed filings supported by exhibits and unrebutted sworn declarations.

The Court awarded $1,035,000 in treble damages over an alleged $345,000 transfer—money that was not paid by DeepGulf, but voluntarily advanced by East Timorese businessmen out of their own income to help cover Moszkowski’s tax burden in France. At the time, Howard owed Moszkowski more than that amount in unpaid salaries. The payment was clearly disclosed in writing to Howard in 2010, 2011, 2012, and 2014.

There was no evidence of harm, standing, or legal theory to support the claim—yet judgment was rendered, again by default and, unavoidably, in absentia.

Moszkowski’s home in France has already been ordered seized by a French court, acting on a default judgment obtained by a co-litigant of DeepGulf, based on disputed funds. The property is scheduled for auction. Even if the Florida judgment is overturned, the French process will not pause or reverse. Appeal will not stay execution.

Moszkowski will be thrown onto the pavement—stripped of shelter, income, and any remaining recourse. His dogs, the only companions who have stood by him, will be euthanized.

The last vestige of a lifetime of effort—technical, intellectual, and moral—will be extinguished.

He appeals not for pity, but for justice, in what may be the final moment before irrevocable loss.
